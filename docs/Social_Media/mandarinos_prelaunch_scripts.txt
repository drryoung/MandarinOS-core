================================================== POST 1 — THE
EMOTIONAL VIRAL HOOK Theme: “I’ve spent thousands and still can’t speak
Chinese.” ==================================================

Imagine spending over $7,000 trying to learn Chinese…

Having a Chinese wife…

Living with a native speaker every single day…

And still not being able to properly hold a conversation in Chinese.

Because that’s literally me.

Over the past few years I’ve done everything people tell you to do.

I paid $4,500 for coaching programs. I flew to China for immersion
courses. I bought Pimsleur Levels 1 through 5. I paid for Duolingo,
SuperChinese, Mandarin Blueprint, MaaYot, Langua—basically every serious
app I could find.

And despite all of that…

I still hit the same wall.

I can maybe say one or two sentences…

Then my brain freezes.

I panic.

And eventually I end up saying:

“Sorry, my Chinese isn’t very good.”

Which is honestly embarrassing at this point.

Because how can someone spend this much time, this much money, have this
much exposure…

and STILL not speak?

And I know I’m not the only one.

Because I keep seeing the same story everywhere:

people who’ve studied for years…

who know tons of vocabulary…

who can read basic Chinese…

who understand grammar…

but the moment an actual conversation starts—

they completely fall apart.

And after years of frustration, I think I’ve finally figured out why.

The problem is not that we’re lazy.

The problem is not that we need more discipline.

The problem is not even that Chinese is “just too hard.”

The real problem…

is that most people are being taught the wrong skill.

If that sounds like your experience, follow me—

because in the next few posts I’m going to explain exactly why I think
almost every major Chinese learning method is failing intermediate
learners.

================================================== POST 2 — “The
Vocabulary Trap” Theme: Why learning more words never seems to solve
speaking ==================================================

So after I made my last video, I kept asking myself:

If I’ve spent years studying Chinese…

if I know hundreds—maybe thousands—of words…

why do I still freeze in conversation?

And I think I finally realised something that gave me a massive aha
moment.

Knowing words is not the same thing as being able to speak.

That sounds obvious…

but most language learners don’t actually internalise that.

Because think about it—

if vocabulary alone made you conversational…

then every intermediate learner should be fluent by now.

But they’re not.

Most intermediates know enough words to survive basic conversations.

The real problem is:

when someone asks you something unexpectedly…

your brain suddenly has to:

understand what they said, search your memory, build a sentence, check
grammar, pronounce it correctly,

all in about two seconds…

while another human is staring at you waiting.

That’s not vocabulary knowledge anymore.

That’s performance under pressure.

It’s the difference between:

knowing how basketball works…

and actually playing basketball in a real game.

You can memorise rules all day—

but until you train under pressure,

you’ll panic the moment the ball comes to you.

And I think this is where most Chinese learners get stuck.

We’re told:

“Just learn more vocab.”

“Do more flashcards.”

“Memorise more words.”

But that’s like telling someone who can’t play basketball:

“Maybe if you memorise more rules, you’ll stop panicking.”

No.

The issue isn’t knowledge anymore.

The issue is that we’ve never trained rapid retrieval under
conversational pressure.

That was a huge aha moment for me.

Because it made me realise:

I don’t necessarily have a knowledge problem.

I may have a training problem.

And I think that’s true for a lot of intermediate learners.

================================================== POST 3 — “Why Apps
Secretly Don’t Teach Speaking” Theme: Recognition vs Generation
==================================================

Once I realised vocabulary wasn’t the main issue…

I started looking at all the apps I’d used and asking:

why haven’t these actually fixed my speaking?

And then I had another huge aha moment:

Most Chinese apps are not designed to teach speaking.

They’re designed to teach recognition.

Think about what almost every app makes you do:

match the sentence, tap the translation, fill in the blank, reorder the
words, review flashcards.

And don’t get me wrong—

that stuff has value.

But notice what all of those exercises have in common:

the answer is already in front of you.

You’re not generating language from scratch.

You’re recognising it.

It’s basically multiple choice.

And recognition feels amazing because it tricks you into thinking:

“Wow, I know this!”

But then real life happens.

A Chinese person asks you something unexpected.

There’s no multiple choice.

There’s no hints.

There’s no sentence bank.

Suddenly your brain has to produce Chinese from nothing.

And you panic.

Because recognising language and generating language are completely
different skills.

It’s like the difference between:

recognising the right answer on a driving theory test…

and actually driving on the motorway in traffic.

One is passive knowledge.

The other is live performance.

And I think that’s why so many learners plateau.

They get really good at recognising Chinese…

but never properly train generating Chinese.

That’s why people can finish app after app after app…

and still struggle the second a real conversation starts.

================================================== POST 4 — “Why
Immersion Doesn’t Magically Fix It” Theme: Why “just talk more” advice
is incomplete ==================================================

Then whenever people hear this struggle,

they always give the same advice:

“Just speak more.”

“Just immerse yourself.”

“Just practice with natives.”

But here’s what frustrates me:

I literally have a Chinese wife.

I have daily access to a native speaker.

I have more immersion than most learners ever get.

And even then…

I still struggled massively.

So clearly immersion alone is not enough.

And I think I finally understand why.

Because immersion assumes something people never talk about:

it assumes you already have enough confidence and conversational
structure to survive inside the immersion.

If you don’t…

immersion just becomes overwhelming.

Think about what actually happens:

someone says something fast…

you partly miss it…

panic kicks in…

you start mentally translating…

your confidence drops…

then you just switch back to English.

That’s not productive immersion.

That’s survival mode.

It’s like throwing someone who can barely swim into the deep end and
saying:

“Just keep swimming, bro.”

Maybe eventually they figure it out…

but more likely they panic.

And I think that’s what happens with most intermediate learners.

We’re not bad at Chinese because we lack exposure.

We’re bad because nobody teaches us how to survive conversation when
things go wrong.

Nobody teaches:

how to stall for time, how to recover when confused, how to redirect,
how to simplify, how to keep momentum going.

So immersion becomes stress instead of growth.

================================================== POST 5 — “The Missing
Skill Nobody Teaches” Theme: Introduce your core thesis / bridge to your
app ==================================================

So after years of frustration…

I think I’ve finally figured out what’s actually missing.

The problem is not:

lack of vocabulary.

The problem is not:

lack of immersion.

The problem is not:

lack of discipline.

The real missing skill is:

nobody teaches you how to manage a conversation when your Chinese is
imperfect.

Think about native conversations.

Real communication is messy.

People:

ask follow-up questions, redirect topics, stall when thinking, clarify
misunderstandings, buy time when confused.

These are actual conversation skills.

But language learning apps mostly pretend conversation is just:

knowing words + grammar.

It’s not.

Conversation is a skill in itself.

And once I realised that…

I became obsessed with one question:

What if instead of teaching Chinese like an academic subject…

we trained it like a practical skill?

What if we trained the actual mechanics of conversation itself?

That idea has basically consumed me for months.

And I’ve been building something around that concept.

Soon I’m going to publicly test it on myself for 10 days…

and document whether this approach can finally break my plateau.

Because honestly—

if this works…

I think it could completely change how intermediate learners approach
Chinese.
